The technology

Think of a conventional speaker like a floodlight — it throws sound everywhere, in all directions, with no control over where it lands. Metaxis is a spotlight. Using an array of speakers working together with precision signal processing, we steer sound beams to specific locations in a space.

The technology controls both the horizontal and vertical direction of sound, so it arrives at the listener's position from a specific angle — which also means what you hear matches what you see. There's no mismatch between the source of the sound and the experience of it.

In simple terms: the sound follows the person, not the other way around. And nobody around them hears it.

This is one of the core technical challenges — and one of the key things our R&D phase is focused on solving at scale. The directional beamforming technology is engineered to minimise sound spill between zones by focusing energy precisely on the target area and reducing it everywhere else.

The system accounts for the acoustic properties of the space — reflections, reverberation, and room shape — and calibrates accordingly. Each zone is effectively acoustically isolated, even though they're running simultaneously in the same room.

This is also why the pilot phase matters: we refine the calibration in a real venue with a real audience to make the isolation as clean as possible before we scale.

Directional and spatial audio technology is well-established — it's used in museums, theme parks, automotive systems and immersive entertainment worldwide. Companies like Holosonics and Panphonics have been delivering directional speaker installations for over a decade.

What Metaxis brings that doesn't currently exist is the full-service model: the consultancy, creative direction, multilingual content layer, backend CMS, and ongoing support — all under one roof, built around inclusive design as the primary goal rather than a secondary consideration.

Our Phase 1 pilot is specifically designed to prove that this integrated model works in practice, in a real venue, with a real diverse audience.

The hardware is a speaker array system — typically ceiling or wall-mounted — alongside a processing unit that handles the signal routing and zone management. The exact configuration depends entirely on the size and acoustic profile of the space.

Installation disruption is deliberately kept minimal. We design for spaces that need to keep operating — museums, galleries, cultural venues — so the installation process is planned around the venue's schedule and can typically be completed outside of opening hours.

The backend content management system runs separately and can be accessed and updated remotely by venue staff without any technical expertise.

Business model

Metaxis operates on a three-stream revenue model:

1. Consultancy & design fee — paid upfront by the venue for the scoping, creative development and technical planning phase.

2. Installation fee — a one-off project fee covering hardware, build and commissioning.

3. Ongoing licensing & support — a recurring annual or monthly fee for content management, system updates, technical support and access to the backend CMS. This is the long-term revenue engine, and what makes the model scalable.

As we scale, the licensing model becomes increasingly valuable — venues pay to keep their experience current, and we build a recurring income stream rather than relying on one-off projects.

A typical Metaxis contract has two stages. The first is a project contract covering consultancy, content creation and installation — priced based on the scale and complexity of the venue. The second is an ongoing service agreement covering content updates, system maintenance and licensing access.

The ongoing agreement is where the business really lives. It creates a long-term relationship with each venue rather than a transactional one, and it means our revenue grows as our installed base grows — without having to win new projects at the same rate.

Installation timelines depend on the scale of the space, but a typical single-room installation can be completed within two to five days, planned around the venue's operating schedule to minimise disruption.

Pricing is bespoke and scoped per venue. During Phase 1 we are working with a pilot partner at a reduced rate in exchange for real-world data, feedback and a case study that validates the model for future commercial contracts.

This is one of Metaxis's key differentiators. Every installation comes with a custom backend content management system that allows venue staff to update, swap and manage their audio content without any technical knowledge — think of it like a simple dashboard.

Want to add a new language? Change the audio for a new exhibition? Update the content for a seasonal event? All of this can be done remotely, without any on-site technical visit. The system is designed to keep the experience current and evolving without ongoing cost or complexity for the venue.

Competition

There are players in adjacent spaces — audio technology companies like Holosonics and Panphonics who make directional speaker hardware; AV integrators who install systems; and accessibility consultants who advise venues. But nobody is doing all three together, with inclusive design as the founding principle.

Consultants don't build. Builders don't consult. Technology companies don't create content or manage ongoing relationships. The market is fragmented, and venues fall through the gaps.

Metaxis is the only company approaching this as a complete, end-to-end system — from the initial consultation through to content, installation, and long-term management — with belonging, not just accessibility, as the goal.

They could — and some do. But buying hardware is only the first step. Without the right calibration for the specific space, without multilingual content created and managed for their audience, without visitor tracking to understand who is walking through the door, and without an ongoing system to keep the experience current — the hardware alone delivers a fraction of the value.

It's like buying a camera and expecting a photography studio. The technology is the enabler. Metaxis is the whole system.

Off-the-shelf solutions also typically serve one purpose — directional advertising or single-language audio guides. Metaxis is designed for simultaneous multilingual, multi-zone, personalised experiences — which requires a fundamentally different approach to both the technology and the content layer.

The ask

The £20,000 is scoped specifically against the deliverables of Phase 1 — a real pilot installation with a real venue and audience. The breakdown is:

50% R&D — sound steering systems and language technology development
20% Pilot installation and venue partnership
15% Content creation and CMS build
10% Safeguarding, accessibility testing and refinement
5% Operations and programme management

This is the minimum required to produce a proof of concept that is rigorous enough to open the door to licensing conversations and further investment. It is not a number chosen arbitrarily — it is the cost of doing Phase 1 properly.

Success at the end of Phase 1 is one venue with a fully operational Metaxis installation, demonstrating simultaneous multilingual audio zones working without devices, with measurable visitor satisfaction and engagement data.

More specifically: visitor feedback confirming the experience of equal access across language groups; venue feedback confirming ease of content management; and quantitative data on zone accuracy and sound isolation — all packaged as a case study that makes the commercial case for Phase 2 licensing conversations.

That case study is the asset. It is what turns the pilot into a template and the template into a scalable licensing model.

Year one revenue comes from two sources. First, the pilot venue pays a consultancy and installation fee — at a reduced pilot rate in exchange for data and case study rights. Second, the ongoing service agreement begins immediately post-installation, generating the first recurring licensing income.

By the end of year one, the goal is one paying venue on an active service agreement, a published case study, and two to three additional venues in active contract conversations — which becomes the foundation for Phase 2 expansion.
